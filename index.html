Project 01

Accelera Tire Testing Data Acquisition System

Senior Design Project Finalist | Multisensor Drift-Vehicle DAQ System

As part of a Senior Design Project Finalist team, I contributed to the design, integration, and evaluation of a multisensor data acquisition system mounted to a Nissan 240SX S13 drift car. The system combined onboard and offboard computing with subsystems for tire pressure, tire temperature, and shock travel related measurement so the team could capture, transmit, and review vehicle data in a usable engineering workflow.

System Integration Supported a fully integrated architecture linking sensors, onboard electronics, and offboard data review.
Subsystem Work Contributed directly to the Tire Pressure Sensor and Slip Angle portions of the project report and design work.
Team Leadership Supported project coordination, communication, and execution while contributing to system evaluation and subsystem development.
  • Worked on a senior design system that combined DAQ hardware, software, communications, and multiple sensing subsystems for drifting applications.
  • Served in a team lead role, helping manage communication, coordination, and progress across the senior design effort.
  • Contributed to technical work documented in the Tire Pressure Sensor, Slip Angle Sensor, Glossary, System Evaluation, Tolerance and Sensitivity, and Conclusions sections of the final report.
  • Used design-analysis methods such as weighted decision matrices, Pugh matrices, and FMEA-informed risk thinking across subsystem development.
  • Supported system evaluation through inspection-based verification and structured testing, including stationary, vibration, distance, and tire-sensor accuracy checks.
  • Helped translate subsystem concepts into schematics, CAD-supported packaging decisions, and final integrated-system documentation.
  • Presented the project as part of a Senior Design Finalist team, reinforcing both technical execution and communication quality.

Project 02

Rear Wing Simulation and Validation Study

Finite Volume, Static Structural, and Vibration Analysis

Designed and evaluated a custom ABS rear wing using an integrated CFD, structural, and vibration simulation workflow to determine whether the concept could safely generate useful aerodynamic downforce at highway speeds.

Simulated aerodynamic loading across an angle-of-attack sweep and transferred those results into static and modal studies to verify stiffness, stress performance, and flutter resistance. Results showed the wing produced measurable downforce while maintaining stresses well below material limits and a first natural frequency above the target threshold.

40 m/s Freestream speed representing highway-speed aerodynamic loading conditions.
329 N Peak reported downward force used to inform the structural loading case.
42.669 Hz First natural frequency exceeded the flutter-avoidance target, confirming structural stability margin.
  • Ran a CFD angle-of-attack sweep in SolidWorks Flow Simulation to characterize lift, drag, and downforce behavior across operating conditions.
  • Transferred aerodynamic loading into a static structural study to evaluate stress and displacement under simulated high-speed loading.
  • Verified that the reported maximum stress remained below the stated ABS yield-strength range, supporting structural feasibility.
  • Performed a vibration study to evaluate natural frequencies and mode shapes with fixed mounting-tab boundary conditions.
  • Cross-checked results against analytical estimates and published reference studies on comparable rear-wing and spoiler geometries.
  • Used the final study to determine that the structure met stiffness and flutter targets, while the selected airfoil geometry was not ideal for motorsports-level aerodynamic performance.

Engineering Conclusion

While the structure met stress and vibration requirements, aerodynamic performance suggested the selected airfoil profile was better suited to general-use validation than motorsports-level downforce generation. Future iterations would focus on higher-performance airfoil geometry, expanded angle-of-attack testing, and additional aerodynamic validation against application-specific operating conditions.
